**CHAPTER 1: Fireline Safety**

**Concept 1 – Physical Fitness Benchmarks**  
 **Numeric‐calculation item**  
 A rookie’s practice run of 1.5 miles took 12 minutes 30 seconds. Fireline fitness guidelines say that to work safely for long shifts you must finish that distance in **11 minutes 40 seconds** or faster.  
 *How many seconds does the rookie need to cut to meet the standard? Show your working and final answer with units.*

Answer: 50 s

Explanation Recommended time \= 11 min 40 s = 700 s. Current time \= 12 min 30 s = 750 s. Difference = 750 s – 700 s \= 50 s. Meeting this aerobic benchmark proves the firefighter’s body can move enough oxygen to muscles during prolonged, high‑heat assignments, reducing fatigue‑linked errors (p. 4).

**Concept 2 – Wearing a Second Protective Layer**  
 **Multiple‑choice item**  
 Field crews are suiting up before dawn. Which undergarment best completes the required **second layer** beneath a Nomex® shirt and trousers when extreme heat exposure is possible?

A. Lightweight polyester athletic top  
 B. Long‑sleeved 100 % cotton or merino‑wool shirt  
 C. Short‑sleeved nylon blend T‑shirt  
 D. Moisture‑wicking rayon tank top

Answer: B – long‑sleeved cotton or merino‑wool shirt

Explanation A natural‑fiber long sleeve creates an insulating air gap and will not melt, so it slows heat transfer and wicks sweat away, buying survival time during flare‑ups.  
 • A – Polyester can melt onto skin (misconception: “high‑tech fabric \= safer”).  
 • C – Nylon also melts and lacks arm coverage (misconception: “short sleeves stay cooler”).  
 • D – Rayon tank leaves forearms/neck exposed (misconception: “any moisture‑wicking top works”). (p. 17)

**CHAPTER 2: Fire Weather**

**Concept 1 – Wind’s role in fire spread**  
 *Format – Multiple‑choice*  
 **Question (≈55 words)**  
You are posted on a gentle hillside when a steady 15‑mph breeze begins blowing straight toward unburned brush ahead of the flames. Thinking about how wind changes a fire’s behaviour, which single effect will most directly speed the fire’s advance in the next few minutes?  
 A. Extra oxygen cools the flames  
 B. Flames lean over and pre‑heat new fuel  
 C. Humid air from the breeze dampens fine fuels  
 D. Wind pushes smoke downward, smothering sparks

**Answer:** B – flames lean over and pre‑heat new fuel

**Explanation**  
 Wind has the greatest influence on wildland fire behaviour because it bends flames, dries and pre‑heats the fuel in front, and carries embers ahead.  
 *Distractor rebuttals* – A: oxygen increases heat, not cooling; C: wind usually lowers, not raises, humidity; D: wind lifts – not settles – smoke. (pp. 72‑74)

---

**Concept 2 – Temperature vs. relative humidity**  
 *Format – True/False*  
 **Question (≈45 words)**  
 Field crews notice the thermometer climbing from 70 °F to 90 °F between late morning and early afternoon on a clear day. According to basic fire‑weather relationships, does such a 20‑degree rise normally cut the relative humidity roughly in half, making fine fuels far easier to ignite?

**Answer:** True

**Explanation**  
 For every 20 °F temperature increase, air’s moisture‑holding capacity doubles, so its relative humidity (the percent of that capacity actually filled) typically drops by about 50 %. A falling RH below 30 % signals rapidly increasing fire danger. (pp. 90‑93)

---

**Concept 3 – Dry adiabatic lapse rate**  
 *Format – short-response (definition)*  
 **Question (≈42 words)**  
When fire‑weather forecasters calculate how a parcel of perfectly dry air cools as it rises up a mountain, they use a standard “dry adiabatic lapse rate.” State that rate in degrees Fahrenheit per thousand‑foot gain so you can compare it with real balloon‑soundings in the field.

**Answer:** Approximately 5.5 °F per 1,000 ft (about 3 °C per 300 m).

**Explanation**  
 Dry air expands and cools at a predictable 5.5 °F per 1,000 ft. Comparing this benchmark with actual balloon data lets firefighters judge whether the atmosphere is stable (cooling less) or unstable (cooling more), a key to spotting potential surges in fire behaviour. (pp. 95‑97)

---

**Concept 4 – Inversion‐related thermal belt**  
 *Format – Fill‑in‑the‑blank*  
 **Question (≈44 words)**  
 After sunset on a clear, calm night, crews patrolling a mountain observe that the middle third of the slope stays noticeably warmer and drier than the valley below or the ridge above. This consistently snug band, caused by a nighttime inversion, is called the \_\_\_\_\_\_.

**Answer:** thermal belt

**Explanation**  
 Cool air drains into valleys while warmer air under the inversion settles mid‑slope, forming a “thermal belt.” Fuels there remain driest overnight, so fire can stay active longer and reignite earlier next day, affecting line placement and patrol priorities. (pp. 101‑103)

---

**Concept 5 – Thunderstorm downdraft hazard**  
 *Format – Brief scenario (38 words)*  
 Dark cumulus towers appear upwind; virga hangs and dust suddenly billows at canyon bottoms. What specific wind phenomenon should crews expect to strike the fireline within minutes, bringing sudden, erratic 25‑60 mph gusts?

**Answer:** A thunderstorm downdraft / gust front (microburst)

**Explanation**  
 Mature thunderstorms release cooled, dense air that plunges to the ground then fans outward as strong, shifting winds. These gust fronts can reverse fire spread, loft embers far ahead, and threaten firefighter safety well before any rain arrives. (pp. 30‑33, 31‑32)

**CHAPTER 3: Topography and Fuels**

#### **1  Slope accelerates fire spread**

**Format:** Multiple‑choice  
 **Question (≈55 words)**  
 Imagine a small grass fire starting halfway up a 35‑degree hillside on a calm day. Within minutes, flames rush uphill far faster than you expected, even though there is no wind. Why does the incline itself make the fire race upward so quickly? Choose the best explanation below.

A. Thinner air at higher elevation cools the flames  
 B. The slope tips unburned grasses closer to rising heat, so they ignite sooner  
 C. Gravity pulls burning embers downhill, which somehow speeds fire uphill  
 D. Steep ground blocks sunlight, keeping fuels drier

**Answer:** B – the slope “presents” fuels to the flame front

**Instructional explanation**  
 On a slope, flames and hot gases rise vertically while the ground angles upward, lifting the next layer of vegetation closer to that heat. Pre‑heating and ignition happen faster, so spread accelerates; the steeper the slope, the faster the run.  
 *Distractor rebuttals*   
 A – Cooler air would slow, not speed, burning.  
 C – Rolling embers move downslope and do not boost uphill spread.  
 D – Shade reduces heating; steep sunny slopes actually receive **more** radiation, not less.

---

#### **2  “Ladder” fuels let fire climb**

**Format:** True/False  
 **Question (≈45 words)**  
 During mop‑up you notice shrubs, low branches, and young pines forming a continuous “staircase” from knee height into the canopy. A supervisor says those plants could let today’s surface fire leap into the crowns if the wind shifts. True or False: that description correctly names ladder fuels.

**Answer:** True

**Instructional explanation**  
 Limbs, brush, and small trees that connect ground fuels to treetops are called ladder fuels because they allow flames to “climb” upward, dramatically increasing intensity and control problems.

---

#### **3  Fuel‑moisture time lag**

**Format:** Fill‑in‑the‑blank  
 **Question (≈42 words)**  
 A crew briefing reminds you that dead grass can take on or lose moisture very quickly, while a large log reacts far more slowly. In standard fuel‑moisture categories, dead grass is a **\_\_\_‑hour** fuel, meaning it needs about that many hours to reach two‑thirds moisture equilibrium.

**Answer:** 1

**Instructional explanation**  
 Dead grass (\<¼ inch diameter) is classified as a 1‑hour fuel; only one hour of drier or moister air changes its moisture content significantly. Logs (\>3 inches) are 1 000‑hour fuels and respond hundreds of times more slowly.

#### **4  Dominant heat‑transfer process**

**Format:** Multiple‑choice  
 **Question (≈50 words)**  
 Picture standing above a campfire: you feel a strong upward blast of hot air that can loft sparks into nearby brush. In wildland fires, which method of heat transfer is chiefly responsible for pre‑heating fuels, creating spot fires, and pushing flames into tree crowns?

A. Conduction  
 B. Convection  
 C. Radiation  
 D. Photosynthesis

**Answer:** B – convection

**Instructional explanation**  
 Convection moves heat as rising columns of super‑heated air, smoke, and gases. These columns dry and ignite fuels ahead of the flame front and can transport burning embers long distances, making convection the primary driver of wildfire spread.  
 *Distractor rebuttals*   
 A – Conduction through soil or wood is slow and minor in wildfires.  
 C – Radiation warms fuels but is less important than moving hot air.  
 D – Photosynthesis is unrelated to heat transfer.

#### **5  Aspect and timing**

**Format:** Brief scenario (34 words)  
 *Scenario:* 2 p.m., mid‑July. You must pick a safe work location. One slope faces north; another faces southwest and is covered in light, sun‑baked grass. Which aspect will likely burn most intensely right now?

**Answer:** The southwest‑facing slope

**Instructional explanation**  
 South‑ and southwest‑facing slopes receive the day’s strongest solar heating, leaving their light, flashy fuels hottest and driest by early afternoon, so fire intensity peaks there at this time.

### **Concept 1 – Sizing a Control Line**

**Format  Numeric calculation**

**Question**  
 You are building first‑attack line through brush that averages four feet tall on level ground with only light wind. To keep flames and heat from jumping the bare‑earth strip you will scrape, you must follow the standard width rule used on wildfires. Using that rule, what is the narrowest width, in feet, that the strip must be?

**Answer**  
 6 ft

**Instructional explanation**  
 *Formulas & working*

1. Standard rule: minimum width = 1.5 × fuel height

2. 1.5 × 4 ft = 6 ft, matching the 4‑6 ft guideline for medium brush.  
    A six‑foot strip keeps radiant and convective heat inside the edge, giving crews a safe workspace and reducing rekindle risk (pp. 195‑197).

### **Concept 2 – Anchor Point**

**Format  Definition / short-response**

**Question**  
 Before any digging starts, firefighters pick a solid starting spot so the fire cannot curl behind them. In one clear sentence, name this spot and explain in plain language why beginning there keeps the crew safer.

**Answer**  
 *Anchor point* — a natural or man‑made barrier where fire cannot pass, so starting the line there prevents the flames from sneaking around and flanking firefighters.

**Instructional explanation**

### **Concept 5 – Minimum‑Impact Tactics (M.I.S.T.)**

**Format  Multiple‑choice**

**Question**  
 A crew boss wants to contain a small grass fire inside a national monument while doing the least lasting harm to soil and cultural features. Which action follows the “minimum impact suppression tactics” philosophy?

A. Bulldoze a 20‑ft‑wide bare strip through a historic stone fence  
 B. Park pumps and fuel cans on a creek bank for easy drafting  
 C. Use the existing gravel service road as the control line and burn out from it  
 D. Scrape handline everywhere, then bury all glowing embers under soil

**Answer**

**Instructional explanation**  
 M.I.S.T. calls for choosing natural or existing barriers first, adding only the control needed to stop the fire.  
 *Distractor rebuttals*  
 A – Bulldozing widens disturbance and destroys heritage structures.  
 B – Fuel beside water risks contamination.  
 D – Burying embers can insulate heat and scars soil.  
 Using the road avoids new ground disturbance yet provides a safe, defensible edge (pp. 213‑216).

#### **1\. Numeric‑calculation**

**Question**  
 While mopping‑up, you face a slash pile estimated at 6 000 “units” of burning fuel (treat each unit as the volume of a gallon of water). Fireline wisdom says a fine mist can cool about 300 fuel‑units per unit of water. Using this rule, figure out the minimum gallons you must spray so the pile stops flaming, and show your working.

**Answer:** 20 gallons

**Explanation**  
 *Formula used* Water needed = Fuel volume ÷ 300  
 *Work* 6 000 ÷ 300 = 20.  
 A fine spray maximises droplet surface area, so every gallon cools roughly 300 gallons‑worth of fuel, saving weight and refill time. This economy is vital when water sources are far from the line.

#### **5\. Definition / short-response**

**Question**  
 When crews deliberately ignite the unburned strip between a completed control line and the main fire—removing that fuel so the line is wider, cleaner, and quicker to mop‑up—what is this close‑range tactic called?

**Answer:** Burning‑out (also called firing‑out)

**Explanation**

#### **3\. Elevation & Head Pressure**

*Format – Numeric calculation*

**Question (≈50 words)**  
 An engine is supplying 100 psi to a hose line that climbs 60 ft up a slope before reaching the nozzle. Using the standard head‑pressure rule of 0.43 psi lost per vertical foot, calculate the expected nozzle pressure once the line is charged and steady. Show your work.

**Answer:** 74 psi (rounded)

**Explanation & steps**  
 Formula: head‑loss = 0.43 psi / ft × elevation change.

1. Loss = 0.43 psi / ft × 60 ft = 25.8 psi.

2. Nozzle pressure = pump pressure – loss = 100 psi – 25.8 psi ≈ 74 psi.  
    Understanding head pressure prevents under‑ or over‑pressurizing lines on slopes, ensuring effective streams and firefighter safety.

### **Assessment Item 5 – Numeric calculation**

 While timing the head of a grass fire, you record that it advances **25 feet in 3 minutes** on level ground. Using the chapter’s rate‑of‑spread categories, determine the fire’s class (Low, Moderate, High, or Extreme). Show your work.

#### **1\. Wildland/Urban Setting**

**Format – Multiple‑choice**

**Question (≈70 words)**  
 While scouting a wildfire threatening a foothill community you notice homes sprinkled irregularly through the pine woods; there is no street‑side break between lawns and natural fuel, and some lots sit a few yards apart while others are nearly a quarter‑mile away. Which type of wildland–urban interface setting does this describe?

**Options**  
 A. Interface condition  
 B. Intermix condition  
 C. Occluded condition  
 D. Rural condition

**Answer:** B – Intermix condition

**Explanation**  
 The intermix condition exists when structures are scattered within continuous wildland fuel with no clear demarcation and densities from roughly two per acre to one every forty acres . Correctly recognizing it guides access, resource needs, and mobility tactics (p. 9‑6).

### **Concept 4 – Calculating Percent Slope**

**Question (≥ 40 words)**

**Answer:** 37.5 %
