CONCEPT: LCES (Lookouts, Communications, Escape Routes, Safety Zones)\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 **QUESTION:** In the safety shorthand LCES used on wildland incidents, the “L” stands for \_\_\_\_\_\_.

**Answer:** Lookouts – LCES is a four-part survival checklist: Lookouts, Communications, Escape routes, and Safety zones. Lookouts are the crew’s “eyes,” positioned to spot changes and warn the team. (pp. 36–42).

\[CONCEPT: Fire Shelter — Last-Resort Survival\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 **QUESTION:** True or False: After you deploy your fire shelter in an entrapment, you should try to move to a “better” spot if it looks slightly cooler.

**Answer:** False – Once inside, stay put and keep the shelter sealed to the ground; moving exposes you to lethal heat. Choose a clear, fuel-free site before deployment and hold the shelter down until the front passes. (pp. 22–23).

\[CONCEPT: Heat Stress, Fatigue, and Hydration\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 **QUESTION:** **Numeric scenario.** A 16-hour shift: first 10 h at 1.5 qt/h of sweat loss, next 6 h at 0.8 qt/h. The firefighter drank 12 qt total. (1) How many more quarts are needed to replace estimated losses for the shift? (2) How many hours of sleep should they plan after the shift, following the work–rest rule of thumb?

**Answer:** 7.8 qt and 8 h – (1) Loss \= 10×1.5 \+ 6×0.8 \= 15 \+ 4.8 \= **19.8 qt**; deficit \= 19.8 − 12 \= **7.8 qt**. (2) Sleep ≈ **16/2 \= 8 h** to control fatigue. Replacing fluids and sleep mitigate heat stress and error risk. *Formulas:* Loss \= rate×time; Sleep ≈ 1 h per 2 h worked. (pp. 8–10).

\[CONCEPT: Fire Shelter — Last-Resort Survival\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 **QUESTION:** **Decision MCQ.** You’re in heavy timber; flame front is imminent; an engine cab is nearby. What’s the best survival choice?

**A.** Stay in the cab in heavy fuels; cab plastics off-gas but it’s cooler  
 **B.** Use the engine as a shield but deploy on the ground away from heavy fuels; drop gear, seal and hold the shelter to the ground  
 **C.** Run uphill toward higher ground without deploying  
 **D.** Stand and hold the shelter chest-high to “catch” cooler air

**Answer:** B – In heavy fuels, use the engine as a shield and deploy on cleared ground away from heat sources; drop flammables, seal/hold the shelter, and stay inside. \[CONCEPT: LCES — Escape Routes Measured in Time\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 **QUESTION:** **Numeric decision.** Trigger point gives 4 minutes from warning to flame front. Crew pace when tired is 2 mph. Two options: Route A is 0.6 mi on a dozer line (downhill); Route B is 0.4 mi on a narrow trail (uphill). Which meets the trigger, and what should you do?

**Answer:** Neither – At 2 mph, time/mi \= 30 min ⇒ A: 0.6×30 \= **18 min**; B: 0.4×30 \= **12 min**. Both exceed 4 min. You must shorten the distance, create a faster route, and avoid uphill routes when possible. Escape routes are measured in **minutes/seconds**, planned with fatigue in mind. (pp. 68–70, 75–76).

**\[CONCEPT:** Winds & Fire Spread **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Easy **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** On a grass fire, your lookout reads a 12-mph wind at 20 feet, but flame lengths are only 3 feet. Which wind measurement best predicts the direction the flame front will actually move and is used to calculate rate of spread?  
 **A.** General wind from the regional forecast  
 **B.** 20-ft surface wind  
 **C.** Mid-flame wind at about half the flame height  
 **D.** Winds aloft measured by a weather balloon  
 **Answer:** C – Mid-flame wind. It is the wind acting where combustion occurs and is the driver for flame-front movement and spread calculations. *Distractors:* A is too coarse for fireline decisions; B can differ markedly from wind inside the fuelbed; D often differs in speed/direction from surface flow. (Winds: definitions and effects, pp. 72–80.)

**\[CONCEPT:** Relative Humidity & Temperature **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Easy **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** True or False: As the afternoon temperature rises, relative humidity typically rises too, making fine fuels moister.  
 **Answer:** False – As temperature goes up, relative humidity goes down, drying fine fuels and increasing fire behavior potential. (Relationship of temperature and RH, pp. 90–95.)

**\[CONCEPT:** Inversion Layers & Thermal Belts **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Easy **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** In the early morning, smoke from a valley fire rises, hits a warm layer aloft, flattens, and spreads sideways. That warm layer acting like a cap is called an \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_.  
 **Answer:** Inversion layer. It is a very stable layer where temperature increases with height, limiting vertical motion so smoke spreads horizontally. (Inversions: definition/effects, pp. 101–105.)

**\[CONCEPT:** Winds & Fire Spread **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Medium **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** It’s 1400 on a clear summer day on a sun-heated midslope above a broad valley. Which local wind pattern is most likely at the fireline?  
 **A.** Gentle downslope 2–5 mph  
 **B.** Upslope 3–8 mph, with developing up-valley 10–15 mph influence  
 **C.** Nighttime land breeze 3–10 mph blowing seaward  
 **D.** Calm, because morning inversion persists  
 **Answer:** B – Daytime solar heating drives upslope winds; large valleys also develop stronger up-valley winds by early afternoon. *Distractors:* A describes nighttime flow; C is a coastal night pattern; D inversions usually weaken/break before noon on sunny days. (Slope/valley winds, pp. 72–80, 23–24 outline.)

**\[CONCEPT:** Relative Humidity & Temperature **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Medium **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** At 0600 the temperature is 60 °F with 60% RH. Forecast high is 80 °F. Using the 20-degree rule of thumb, estimate the afternoon RH.  
 **Answer:** \~30% – A 20 °F rise roughly halves RH (≈60% → ≈30%), indicating much drier fuels by afternoon. *Working:* ΔT \= \+20 °F → RH ≈ RH/2. (RH change with temperature, pp. 90–95.)

**\[CONCEPT:** Problem Winds & Fronts/Thunderstorms **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Medium **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** A dry cold front is approaching your division. Which wind evolution is most typical as it passes?  
 **A.** South → Southeast → East, gentle and steady  
 **B.** Southeast → South → Southwest, then a rapid shift to West → Northwest with gusts  
 **C.** North → Northeast, then calm  
 **D.** Steady onshore sea breeze all day  
 **Answer:** B – Cold fronts commonly bring increasing winds and a sharp post-frontal shift to W/NW, often gusty. *Distractors:* A/C don’t reflect the characteristic sharp post-frontal veer; D describes coastal sea breezes, not frontal passage. (Cold front winds, pp. 80–86.)

**\[CONCEPT:** Problem Winds & Fronts/Thunderstorms **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** A maturing thunderstorm is building upwind of an active flank. You are between the fire and the storm, and you see virga and a rolling cloud base. What should you do?  
 **A.** Reposition so you are not between fire and storm; anticipate strong, erratic outflow gusts; suspend firing; brief escape routes  
 **B.** Use the gust front to “push” the fire into your burnout  
 **C.** Drop to the canyon bottom to “hide” from winds  
 **D.** Keep working until you see lightning nearby  
 **Answer:** A – Thunderstorm downdrafts (gust fronts/microbursts) can produce 25–60+ mph, highly erratic winds; being between the fire and storm is especially dangerous. *Distractors:* B risks sudden blow-ups; C funnels winds and can trap crews; D delays action until it’s too late. (Thunderstorm indicators/downdrafts, pp. 83–88.)

**\[CONCEPT:** Atmospheric Stability & Lapse Rate **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** A morning sounding shows 70 °F at 1,000 ft and 58 °F at 3,000 ft. Is the lower atmosphere stable, neutral, or unstable for vertical motion?  
 **Answer:** Unstable – Temperature drop is 12 °F over 2,000 ft → 6 °F/1,000 ft, which is greater than the dry adiabatic 5.5 °F/1,000 ft threshold, favoring vertical motion and more active fire behavior. *Formula/Steps:* Lapse Rate \= |ΔT|/Δz \= 12/2000 \= 0.006 °F/ft \= 6 °F/1,000 ft \> 5.5 → unstable. (Stability and lapse rates, pp. 95–101.)

**\[CONCEPT:** Inversion Layers & Thermal Belts **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** Evening operations near the coast: Santa Ana winds are degrading and a marine layer is pushing inland. The smoke column wavers with sudden calms (“battling winds”). What’s the best call on ignition operations?  
 **A.** Continue burnout; winds will remain steadily offshore  
 **B.** Pause/secure firing until the wind trend stabilizes; expect abrupt 180° shifts during the transition  
 **C.** Accelerate firing to finish before up-valley winds increase  
 **D.** Proceed aggressively; nighttime conditions will be calm  
 **Answer:** B – As foehn flow weakens and marine air intrudes, winds can reverse rapidly, endangering crews and firing plans; suspending firing avoids rapid wind flips. *Distractors:* A/D ignore documented reversals; C speeds operations into the hazard window. (Foehn/marine “battling winds,” pp. 80–86, 101–105.)

**\[CONCEPT:** Winds & Fire Spread **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** You plan indirect line below a thermal belt on a mountain slope for a next-day burnout. Night forecast calls for a strong nighttime inversion and poor humidity recovery. What is your best timing/location adjustment?  
 **A.** Burn at midnight inside the thermal belt to use the “warmest” night air  
 **B.** Shift firing below the thermal belt and wait until after the inversion breaks late morning, when wind/ventilation are predictable  
 **C.** Burn just before dawn while the inversion is strongest to trap smoke  
 **D.** No change; inversions reduce fire behavior everywhere equally  
 **Answer:** B – Thermal belts stay warm/dry at night under inversions, sustaining active fire; after the inversion breaks, winds/ventilation normalize and behavior is more predictable. *Distractors:* A/C keep you in unstable, smoky, or erratic conditions; D is false—effects vary by slope elevation. (Thermal belts and inversion breakdown, pp. 101–105.)

**CHAPTER 3: Topography and Fuels**

\[CONCEPT: Heat Transfer in Wildland Fire\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 QUESTION: You’re standing downwind of a small campfire and feel the heat rising above it. In wildland fires, which heat-transfer process most strongly spreads fire by preheating fuels, lofting embers, and moving flames into tree crowns?  
 A. Conduction  
 B. Convection  
 C. Radiation  
 D. Refraction  
 **Answer:** B – Convection. Heated air rises, carrying hot gases and embers that preheat/ignite unburned fuels and can move fire into crowns; conduction is minor in wildland fuels, and radiation matters but less than convection for spread. (Figure “How heat moves”; pp. 128–129).

\[CONCEPT: Fuel Layers & Ladder Fuels\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 QUESTION: Fill in the blank: “Ladder fuels are the low limbs, shrubs, and small trees that allow a surface fire to \_\_\_\_\_\_ into the crowns of taller trees.”  
 **Answer:** climb. They form a vertical path so a hot surface fire can transition upward into the canopy, greatly increasing intensity and control difficulty. (Ladder fuels definition and effects; pp. 142–143).

\[CONCEPT: Slope Effects on Spread\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 QUESTION: True or False: On a calm day with no wind, a fire burning on a steep uphill slope will generally spread faster than the same fire on flat ground because the slope “presents” fuels to the flame front.  
 **Answer:** True. Slope places fuels closer to rising flames; radiation and convection preheat fuels upslope, increasing rate of spread. (Slope and preheating; pp. 118–121).

\[CONCEPT: Slope Effects on Spread\] \[DIFFICULTY: Medium\]  
 QUESTION: A head fire races uphill and reaches a ridge crest at noon in light winds. As it crosses onto the opposite side, what change in spread is most likely?  
 A. Accelerate further uphill  
 B. Slow and begin to back downslope  
 C. Crown immediately due to radiation  
 D. Reverse and run back down the original slope  
 **Answer:** B – Slow and begin to back downslope. At a ridge, slope reverses; the fire loses upslope preheating and typically backs on the lee side, reducing spread. (Slope reversal behavior; p. 120).

\[CONCEPT: Fuel Moisture & Time-Lag\] \[DIFFICULTY: Medium\]  
 QUESTION: A dry front drops relative humidity from 35% to 20% for four hours. Which fuel class will show the quickest moisture decrease during that window?  
 A. 1-hour fuels (≤¼″)  
 B. 10-hour fuels (¼–1″)  
 C. 100-hour fuels (1–3″)  
 D. 1000-hour fuels (3–8″)  
 **Answer:** A – 1-hour fuels. Smaller, finer fuels exchange moisture with the air fastest (shortest time-lag), so a brief, sharp RH drop dries them most quickly. (Dead fuel moisture exchange & time-lag; pp. 134–138).

\[CONCEPT: Canyons & Chimneys\] \[DIFFICULTY: Medium\]  
 QUESTION: Mid-afternoon in a narrow, V-shaped canyon, a crew proposes direct line mid-slope inside the canyon. Which specific hazard makes this location especially unsafe at that time?  
 A. Cold-air pooling that calms fire behavior  
 B. Strong upslope drafts and spotting to the opposite slope  
 C. Heat conduction through bedrock igniting fuels downslope  
 D. Late-day fog that suppresses convection  
 **Answer:** B – Strong upslope drafts and spotting. Canyons act like chimneys in heat of day, funneling winds upslope and promoting spotting across to the opposite wall—erratic, fast-moving fire behavior. (Canyon/chimney effect; pp. 122–124).

\[CONCEPT: Canyons & Chimneys\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 QUESTION: In a steep, narrow chute (a “chimney”) with dense shrubs beneath a closed canopy, a sudden upslope wind surge produces rapid crown fire spread. Which factor combination best explains this transition?  
 A. Conduction through rock \+ heavy ground fuels  
 B. Convection funneled upslope \+ ladder fuels  
 C. Radiation only from the flaming front  
 D. High live-fuel moisture preventing preheating  
 **Answer:** B – Convection funneled upslope \+ ladder fuels. The chimney accelerates hot updrafts that preheat/ignite elevated fuels; abundant ladder fuels then carry flames into crowns. (Chimney effect; ladder fuels role; pp. 122–124, 142–143).

\[CONCEPT: Fuel Layers & Ladder Fuels\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 QUESTION: You have one saw team for 30 minutes before a forecast wind shift. Along your control line in mixed conifer with tight crown closure and dense understory, which single action best reduces the chance of surface fire transitioning to crown fire?  
 A. Mop up cold ash in the black  
 B. Limb up and remove ladder fuels 10–15 ft out from the line  
 C. Scratch a new line closer to the fire in the canyon bottom  
 D. Widen line only in adjacent light grass  
 **Answer:** B – Remove ladder fuels. Eliminating vertical continuity is the most direct way to prevent surface-to-crown transition under tightening canopies before winds increase. (Vertical continuity & ladder fuels; pp. 141–143).

\[CONCEPT: Fuel Moisture & Time-Lag\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 QUESTION: Which site most clearly meets a dangerous fuel-moisture threshold indicating potential for extreme burning in its dominant size class?  
 A. Site A: Cured grass; RH 30%; 1-hour fuels present  
 B. Site B: Shrubland; 10-hour fuel moisture 8%  
 C. Site C: Timber; 1000-hour fuel moisture 12%  
 D. Site D: Mixed live woody fuels; live fuel moisture high  
 **Answer:** C – Timber with 1000-hour fuel moisture 12%. Be alert for extreme conditions when 1-hr fuels coincide with RH ≤25%, 10-hr fuels ≤7%, or 1000-hr fuels ≤13%; Site C crosses a listed threshold. (Fuel-moisture thresholds; p. 139).

\[CONCEPT: Fire Environment (Weather–Topography–Fuels)\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 **QUESTION:** On an initial size-up, you list weather, topography, and fuels. Which of these is the most variable component that can change fire behavior hour-to-hour and must be watched most closely through the shift?  
 **A.** Topography  
 **B.** Fuels  
 **C.** Weather  
 **D.** Fuel temperature alone  
 **Answer:** C – Weather. Weather (wind, temperature, relative humidity) is the most variable and strongly drives rate/direction of spread and intensity. Rebuttals: A Topography is largely fixed; B Fuels change with space/time but slower than weather; D Fuel temperature is one fuel factor, not the whole driver. pp. 148–151.

\[CONCEPT: Ignition of Wildland Fuels\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 **QUESTION:** You’re testing ignition potential with a drip torch across several fuel beds. Under the same heat source, which fuel situation is most likely to ignite first?  
 **A.** Small, fine fuels that are loosely arranged and dry  
 **B.** Large logs stacked tightly with little air space  
 **C.** Green shrubs at peak growth stage  
 **D.** Cold, shaded fuels damp with morning dew  
 **Answer:** A – Small, fine, loose, dry fuels ignite easiest (less heat needed; air movement aids combustion). Rebuttals: B Compact, large fuels resist ignition; C Live/green fuels have high moisture; D Cold, wet fuels require extra heat to reach ignition. p. 149–151.

\[CONCEPT: Spotting & Firebrands\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 **QUESTION:** True or False: Spotting occurs when burning material is carried ahead of the main fire and, upon landing in receptive fuels, starts new fires.  
 **Answer:** True – Spotting is the transport of firebrands (by popping, convection, wind, or gravity) that can ignite new fires ahead of the front. pp. 151–152.

\[CONCEPT: Spotting & Firebrands\] \[DIFFICULTY: Medium\]

\[CONCEPT: Fire Behavior Characteristics (Rate of Spread & Flame Length)\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 **QUESTION:** A fire’s rate of spread is estimated at 3 chains per hour. One chain \= 66 ft. Your dozer line is 300 ft ahead on flat, uniform fuels. About how long before the head reaches the line (assume no change in conditions)?  
 **Answer:** 1.5 hours (≈1 hr 31 min). Explanation: Convert 3 chains/hr × 66 ft/chain \= 198 ft/hr. Time \= distance / speed \= 300 ft ÷ 198 ft/hr ≈ 1.52 hr. Operationally, this short window argues for alternate anchor/line placement or flanking. (Rate units & chain definition). p. 153\.

Rebuttals: A High live moisture and spacing resist crowning; C Stable, moist conditions suppress vertical development; D Grasses tend to produce little crowning. pp. 163–169, 4-46–4-49 (crown fire factors).

\[CONCEPT: Class A Foam – Proportioning & Use\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 QUESTION: Numeric calculation — Your engine flows 60 gpm of foam solution. For 10 minutes at 0.5% and the next 10 minutes at 0.2%, how many gallons of concentrate are consumed in total?

 **QUESTION:** On a growing wildland incident, which ICS section is responsible for preparing the written Incident Action Plan (IAP) for each operational period and tracking resource/situation status?  
 **A.** Operations Section  
 **B.** Planning Section  
 **C.** Logistics Section  
 **D.** Finance/Administration Section  
 **Answer:** B – Planning Section. The Planning Section prepares the IAP, tracks resource and situation status, and anticipates future needs. Distractors: A manages tactics, not plan prep; C provides support/services; D tracks costs and procurement. (Section II & IV, pp. 389–403).

\[CONCEPT: Incident Facilities\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 **QUESTION:** True or False: A Staging Area is part of the Operations Section and is used to hold resources that are checked in and ready for rapid tactical assignment under the Operations Section Chief.  
 **Answer:** True – Staging Areas are within Operations, managed by a Staging Area Manager, and position ready resources for deployment. (Section III, pp. 395–396, 399).

\[CONCEPT: ICS Core Features & Principles\] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy\]  
 **QUESTION:** Which statement best reflects ICS span of control?  
 **A.** One supervisor should manage exactly five resources.  
 **B.** One supervisor should manage no more than three resources.  
 **C.** Typical effective range is three to seven; one-to-five is recommended.  
 **D.** Any number is acceptable if the supervisor is experienced.  
 **Answer:** C – ICS teaches a typical range of 3–7 with a recommended 1–5 to maintain safe, effective supervision. A/B are too rigid; D ignores proven limits. (Section I & II, pp. 380–381, 389–391).

\[CONCEPT: Resource Typing & Status\] \[DIFFICULTY: Medium\]  
 **QUESTION:** (Multiple-choice scenario) You need five engines of the same type under one leader with common comms to do structure protection along one road segment. What should you request?  
 **A.** A Task Force  
 **B.** A Strike Team  
 **C.** Five Single Resources  
 **D.** A Division  
 **Answer:** B – A Strike Team is a set number of the same kind/type with a leader and common comms. A is a mixed set; C lacks unified leadership/comms; D is a geographic org unit, not a resource order. (Section I & IV, pp. 10-10 to 10-11, 401–402).

\[CONCEPT: Unified Command\] \[DIFFICULTY: Medium\]  
 **QUESTION:** Fill in the blank: Under a Unified Command, there is one \_\_\_\_\_\_ who directs implementation of the Incident Action Plan for all agencies involved.  
 **Answer:** Operations Section Chief. Unified Command sets common objectives; one Operations Section Chief executes the plan to integrate tactics. (Section I & VI, pp. 380–381, 407–409).

\[CONCEPT: Resource Typing & Status\] \[DIFFICULTY: Medium\]  
 **QUESTION:** Short response: An engine is (1) pumping a hose lay on Division A, then (2) moved to Staging after mop-up, then (3) parked for a flat tire. What is its status at each step?  
 **Answer:** (1) Assigned → (2) Available → (3) Out-of-service. Assigned \= committed to a task; Available \= ready in Staging; Out-of-service \= not deployable due to mechanical/personnel limits. (Section I & IV, pp. 10-10 to 10-11, 401–402, 404–405).

\[CONCEPT: ICS Organization & Roles\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]

 **B.** Create two Branches (e.g., North/South) and place divisions/groups under them  
 **C.** Replace divisions with one large Group to simplify supervision

 **Answer:** B – Establish Branches to maintain span of control and organize multiple divisions/groups logically. A exceeds span; C removes needed geographic control; D misunderstands Staging (it remains in Operations). (Section II, pp. 387–391).

\[CONCEPT: ICS Core Features & Principles\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 **QUESTION:** Numeric calculation: Operations must supervise 18 divisions/groups. To keep (i) each Branch Director supervising ≤5 divisions/groups and (ii) the Operations Section Chief supervising ≤5 branches, what is the minimum number of branches you should create?  
 **Answer:** 4 – Create 4 branches.  
 Explanation:  
 • Constraint: ≤5 divisions per Branch → ⌈18 ÷ 5⌉ \= 4 branches.  
 • Ops supervises 4 Branch Directors, which is ≤5.  
 Formulas: ceiling division; span-of-control limits per ICS guidance (typical 3–7, recommended 1–5). (Section II, pp. 387–391).

\[CONCEPT: Major Incident Management (Complex/Area/Unified)\] \[DIFFICULTY: Hard\]  
 **QUESTION:** (Multiple-choice scenario) A dry thunderstorm starts eight small fires within a 20-mile corridor. They’re close enough to share logistics and one IMT can handle them; none is yet large. What organizational approach best fits?  
 **A.** Area Command over eight separate ICS organizations

\[CONCEPT: Situational Awareness & Trigger Points \] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy \]  
 QUESTION: In one sentence, what is a trigger point and why do crews set them?  
 Answer: A trigger point is a pre-set condition (e.g., wind shift, RH or fire reaching a ridge) that, once met, prompts a preplanned action like moving to a safety zone. Explanation: Pre-deciding actions reduces hesitation under stress and builds safe margins into operations. \[pp. 423–424\]

\[CONCEPT: Small vs. Large Fire Operations \] \[DIFFICULTY: Easy \]  
 QUESTION: True or False: Small fires typically spread by direct flame contact, short-range spotting, and rolling material; large fires often move into a “third dimension” with strong vertical plumes that change tactics.  
 Answer: True. Explanation: Small fires behave mainly in two dimensions; large fires add vertical plume dynamics, bringing long-range spotting and different control strategies. \[pp. 433–435\]

**\[CONCEPT:** Contours & Terrain Interpretation **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Easy **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** True or False: On a topographic map, contour lines that are close together indicate a gentle, uniform slope that is easy to walk and slows fire spread.  
 **Answer:** False – Close spacing means a steep slope; steep slopes accelerate uphill runs and are harder to traverse. Widely spaced contours indicate gentler ground. (pp. 466–467, 469\)

**\[CONCEPT:** Compass & Declination **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Easy **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** Fill in the blank: Compass \_\_\_\_\_\_ is the correction applied because a compass points to magnetic north (in Canada) rather than true north at the pole; it varies by location and can change with time.  
 **Answer:** Declination – It’s the angle between magnetic and true north that must be accounted for when navigating. (pp. 462–463, 478–481)

**\[CONCEPT:** Scale & Distance **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Medium **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** Your 1:24,000 topo map shows a dozer push of 3.5 inches along a ridge. What is the ground distance in feet and miles?  
 **Answer:** 7,000 ft (≈1.33 mi) – Work: 3.5 in × 24,000 \= 84,000 in; 84,000 ÷ 12 \= 7,000 ft; 7,000 ÷ 5,280 ≈ 1.33 mi. Uses representative fraction and unit conversion. (pp. 462–464)

**\[CONCEPT:** Slope / Grade Calculation **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Medium **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** A lookout stands at 1,240 ft elevation. A knob upslope is 1,420 ft away by elevation and 0.9 miles away horizontally. What is the percent slope?  
 **Answer:** 3.8% – Work: Vertical rise \= 1,420 − 1,240 \= 180 ft. Horizontal \= 0.9 mi × 5,280 \= 4,752 ft. % slope \= (180 ÷ 4,752) × 100 ≈ 3.8%. (pp. 476–477)

**\[CONCEPT:** Compass & Declination **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Medium **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** From camp you’re told the spot fire is at a true bearing of 112°. Local declination is 15° W. What compass setting should you follow on your magnetic compass?  
 **A.** 97°  
 **B.** 112°  
 **C.** 127°  
 **D.** 15°  
 **Answer:** A – 97°. For west declination, subtract from the true bearing: 112° − 15° \= 97°. B: ignores declination. C: adds instead of subtracts. D: declination value, not a bearing. (pp. 480–481)

**\[CONCEPT:** Scale & Distance **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** You must estimate time for a dozer along a winding road. The map has both a graphic scale and 1:24,000 RF. What’s the quickest accurate way to get the road length from the map?

**\[CONCEPT:** Slope / Grade Calculation (using Contours & Scale) **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** On a 1:24,000 map, Point A is at 1,200 ft and Point B is at 1,520 ft. The contour interval is 40 ft. Map distance A–B is 2.2 inches (straight line). What is the percent slope A→B?  
 **Answer:** 7.3% – Work: Vertical rise \= 1,520 − 1,200 \= 320 ft (8 intervals × 40). Ground distance \= 2.2 in × 24,000 \= 52,800 in \= 4,400 ft. % slope \= (320 ÷ 4,400) × 100 ≈ 7.27% ≈ 7.3%. (pp. 462–464, 465–471, 476–477)

 **Answer:** B – Stop and do not move closer, clearly mark, keep ≥500 ft away, avoid radio transmission near UXO, and report to supervision for proper disposal. A/C/D increase detonation risk. (pp. 514–515).

**\[CONCEPT:** General Guidelines for Initial Haz-Mat Response **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** Multiple-choice scenario: You arrive first to a roadside puddle of unknown chemical with bystanders walking toward it and a breeze pushing vapors across the lane. Which action package best matches recommended guidelines?  
 **A.** Enter quickly to identify the product; reassure bystanders; move fire apparatus closer for lighting  
 **B.** Isolate the area and direct people upwind/upgrade to a safe distance; avoid walking into/through the spill or vapors; maintain comms with supervisor/dispatch  
 **C.** Attempt to neutralize the liquid with dirt while you wait for haz-mat specialists  
 **D.** Ignore until a confirmed placard or shipping paper is found  
 **Answer:** B – First isolate and keep the public away, stay upwind/upgrade, avoid contact/inhalation, and maintain communications; do not rush in or manipulate the product. A/C/D violate “do not touch,” “avoid vapors,” and the need to act defensively early. (pp. 507–509).

**\[CONCEPT:** Hand tool spacing & passing protocol **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Easy **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** Fill in the blank: When you need to move past coworkers who are actively swinging tools on the fireline, you should call out “\_\_\_\_\_\_” and wait until they pause before passing.  
 **Answer:** Coming through\! – This alerts others to stop swinging so you can pass without entering their tool arc; it’s a specific, taught phrase, not casual chatter. p. 15-7;

**\[CONCEPT:** Cutting tools — axe carry basics **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Easy **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** You’re traversing uneven ground with an axe. What is the correct way to carry it to minimize injury risk and maintain control?  
 **A.** Grip the handle end; let the head trail behind with the handle angled up  
 **B.** Hold it at the balance point just behind the head, head facing forward, handle parallel to the ground  
 **C.** Sling it over a shoulder with the blade behind you  
 **D.** Hold the bit in your gloved hand with the edge pointing down  
 **Answer:** B – Holding at the balance point with the head forward keeps control and reduces fatigue; the other methods are unstable or unsafe. Rebuttals: A—poor control; C—exposes others; D—never hold by the bit. p. 15-11;

**\[CONCEPT:** Scraping tools — safe McLeod carry **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Easy **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** When carrying a McLeod along the line, which orientation best reduces the chance of puncture injuries if you fall?  
 **A.** Hoe edge down to cut while walking  
 **B.** Hoe edge up so you won’t fall on the rake tines  
 **C.** Rake tines forward for quick deployment  
 **D.** Blade vertical to fit narrow trails  
 **Answer:** B – Carrying hoe edge up avoids falling onto the tines; punctures are more dangerous than lacerations. Rebuttals: A—creates trip hazard; C—exposes tines; D—doesn’t address injury risk. p. 15-17;

**\[CONCEPT:** Hand tool sharpening — file technique **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Medium **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** You’re showing a rookie how to file a Pulaski. Which guidance is correct for both edge quality and safety?  
 **A.** Use a power grinder; press hard on both strokes  
 **B.** Use a flat mill-bastard file with guard; wear gloves; make long forward strokes, keep a constant angle, release pressure on the backstroke  
 **C.** Short, fast strokes changing angles to “chase” nicks  
 **D.** File toward your body so you can see the bevel  
 **Answer:** B – Forward-only pressure with a guarded mill-bastard file, constant angle, and gloves yields a safe, consistent edge. Rebuttals: A—risks ruining tool; C—inconsistent bevel; D—increases injury risk. p. 15-5;

**\[CONCEPT:** Scraping tools — two-person McLeod team **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Medium **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** Light grass is racing upslope. Two firefighters must cut a quick scratch line with McLeods. Which setup is correct?  
 **A.** Both chop vertically like garden hoes  
 **B.** Lead faces travel with tool behind, scraping edge down; second trails with tool in front, rake tines intertwined atop the first, adding downward pressure  
 **C.** Lead rakes; second scrapes randomly for speed  
 **D.** Both pull backward to keep faces away from heat  
 **Answer:** B – Intertwined tools let the lead provide motion while the rear adds cutting pressure—fast for emergency scratch line. Rebuttals: A—wastes energy; C—uncoordinated; D—awkward/unsafe. p. 15-18;

**\[CONCEPT:** Backpack pumps — diagnosing flow issues **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Medium **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** A rigid backpack tank starts denting inward and the stream weakens even though it’s full. What single check fixes the problem first?  
 **A.** Tighten all hose clamps  
 **B.** Open/clear the air vent in the tank lid  
 **C.** Switch to spray tip for less backpressure  
 **D.** Add fuel to the engine  
 **Answer:** B – A plugged lid vent causes vacuum and tank collapse; clearing it restores flow. (Manual backpumps have no engine.) Rebuttals: A/C—symptoms are vacuum, not leaks; D—N/A. p. 15-28;

**\[CONCEPT:** Felling tools — wedges & lookout **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** While chainsawing a leaning tree, the kerf starts to close. Which choice manages the cut and team safety?  
 **A.** Drive a metal wedge; keep cutting solo to finish fast  
 **B.** Insert plastic wedges; designate a lookout and use a preplanned escape route  
 **C.** Use a wooden wedge; continue without a lookout to reduce noise  
 **D.** Pry the kerf with the bar to avoid carrying wedges  
 **Answer:** B – Plastic wedges prevent chain damage/kickback with saws, and a lookout plus escape route are required risk controls. Rebuttals: A—metal damages chain; C—no lookout is unsafe; D—prying risks bar/kerf failure. p. 15-24 (wedges), p. 15-23 (lookout/escape);

**\[CONCEPT:** Backpack pumps — burn-out support tactics **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** During a burn-out in light grass with frequent spots, which combination best controls spread while conserving water?  
 **A.** Random blasts ahead of the flame; keep walking  
 **B.** Deploy pumps sparingly; use wide fog only  
 **C.** Deploy pumps liberally; sweep parallel to the edge in a coordinated motion; keep nozzle hand steady and pump with the other; add wetting agent to each tank  
 **D.** Aim high arcs over the line to “rain” water down  
 **Answer:** C – Parallel sweeping coordinated with pumping controls the edge; wetting agent stretches water; steady/nozzle hand improves aim. Rebuttals: A—wastes water; B—too little coverage; D—poor placement/splash. p. 15-29–15-30;

**\[CONCEPT:** Hand tools — side-hill movement & spacing **\] \[DIFFICULTY:** Hard **\]**  
 **QUESTION:** Your crew is cutting handline on a steep side-hill. Which set of practices is correct?  
 **A.** Carry tools on uphill side; 6-ft spacing; pass silently to avoid distractions  
 **B.** Carry tools on downhill side; ≥10-ft spacing; announce “Coming through\!” and wait for swings to stop before passing  
 **C.** Carry tools vertically; any spacing works if PPE is worn; pass whenever there’s a gap  
 **D.** Carry tools downhill; 3-ft spacing to stay tight; pass quickly without stopping  
 **Answer:** B – Downhill carry prevents landing on a sharp edge if you fall; maintain at least 10 feet between workers; use the taught pass-through call and wait. Rebuttals: A/C/D—each violates one or more safety rules. p. 15-7–15-8;
